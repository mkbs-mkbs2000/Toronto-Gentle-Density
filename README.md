# Toronto-Gentle-Density
This is adapted from an original coursework submitted for CASA0007 Quantitative Methods (2025/2026) by Muhammad Khalis, with the full R code accessible via the "Toronto Gentle Density Code.Rmd" file.

The analysis initially focused on two questions. First, using HDBSCAN, are ADUs randomly scattered across the city, or do they cluster in particular areas? Second, using OLS regression, which factors are linked to higher ADU uptake across the city?

### Data Processing
The ADU dataset was assembled from the City of Toronto’s building permit records, using both active permits and cleared permits, and retaining only those issued from January 1, 2022 until December 31, 2025 for rear-yard suites, laneway suites and new secondary suites. Since permit revisions can generate multiple records for the same project, permits were deduplicated by permit number so that each project was counted only once. These records were then linked to the city’s address point dataset through a shared address identifier. Once geocoded, the permits were assigned to Toronto neighbourhoods. This created the basic geography for the rest of the analysis: the count of ADU permits in each neighbourhood. The raw counts are then normalised as a proportion out of total eligible housing stock in each neighbourhood - the sum of all single-detached houses, semi-detached houses, row houses and duplexes.

To capture other neighbourhood data to facilitate OLS regression, 2021 Census data that was aggregated by the City of Toronto to neighbourhood level was utilised. Variables from the Census covered three broad dimensions: housing stock, demographics and housing pressures. Housing stock included the mix of dwelling types as well as the age of homes. Demographic variables included household size, family structure, visible minority share and mobility-related measures. Housing pressure variables included income, inequality, mortgage strain and housing suitability.

Another dimension that is brought in for consideration in the regression analysis is accessibility, which includes measures for cycling, walking, public transit in peak hours and public transit in off-peak hours. Statistics Canada provided these access measures at the dissemination block level, not at Toronto neighbourhood level. Thus, a block-to-neighbourhood relationship file also had to be developed. Dissemination blocks were first linked to census tracts using the official relationship file, and census tracts were then spatially intersected with Toronto neighbourhood boundaries. Where tract and neighbourhood boundaries did not line up neatly (a common problem since the geographic data came from different sources), I kept the largest intersected area for each tract. This provided a workable way to translate block-level accessibility into neighbourhood-level measures. Accessibility values were then summarised to neighbourhood level using the median rather than the mean, so as to give a more typical value and is less sensitive to outliers.

### Are ADUs Randomly Scattered Across the City
It should first be examined whether ADU permits were spatially clustered to begin with. Ripley’s K statistic showed that the observed point pattern departed from complete spatial randomness and remained more clustered than expected up to roughly two kilometres. In plain terms, ADUs were not appearing evenly across the city. They were tending to emerge in pockets. To identify those pockets more clearly, HDBSCAN, a density-based clustering method that can detect groups of different sizes and shapes while leaving some points unclustered, is then applied. This revealed seven ADU clusters across Toronto, ranging from relatively small groupings to much larger concentrations. Two stood out in particular: one stretching west of downtown toward High Park and Lawrence Avenue, and another extending eastward from the Don River into Scarborough. These clusters suggested that ADU uptake may be shaped by more than parcel-level decision-making alone.

### Which Factors Are Linked to Higher ADU Uptake Across the City
The four broad dimensions for regression resulted in a large set of 34 potential explanatory variables. Rather than place all of them directly into a regression, the variables are reduced using principal components analysis (PCA). This made the models more manageable and helped avoid excessive overlap between correlated indicators.

Accessibility collapsed strongly into a single component representing general non-auto accessibility. Housing type variables were reduced into three components capturing different built-form mixes, including a strong single-detached/duplex dimension and contrasts between semi-detached and row-house areas. Construction period variables were reduced into two components, one associated with older, pre-1960 housing stock and another associated more strongly with housing built from the 1960s to the 1980s. Household size variables also condensed into a smaller number of interpretable dimensions.

The dependent variable, ADU rate, included many zero values, since some neighbourhoods had no recent ADU permits at all. To keep those cases in the analysis, the rates were transformed using log1p rather than a standard logarithm. Several skewed explanatory variables were also log-transformed, including visible minority share, mover rates, single-parent households, couples with children, income, inequality, mortgage burden and housing unsuitability.

Regression models were then built step by step with the aim of maximising the R-Squared score but also adhering to the OLS assumptions. The results summary table is as shown!

The final model explained a meaningful portion of variation in ADU uptake across neighbourhoods, though far from all of it. That remaining unexplained variation mattered, because the residuals were not spatially random. Moran’s I indicated significant spatial autocorrelation in the model errors, meaning that nearby neighbourhoods still resembled one another more than the OLS model could account for. In effect, there was still a missing spatial process. Lagrange multiplier tests suggested that a spatial lag model was more appropriate than a spatial error model, so the model is re-estimated using a spatial lag specification with queen contiguity weights.

This changed the interpretation in an important way. The spatial lag coefficient was large and statistically significant, indicating a strong spillover effect: ADU uptake in one neighbourhood was closely associated with uptake in neighbouring areas. Once this surrounding influence was accounted for, general accessibility was no longer statistically significant. By contrast, built form and housing age remained important. Neighbourhoods with more single-detached and duplex housing, and those with older housing stock, continued to show stronger ADU uptake even after spatial dependence was introduced.
